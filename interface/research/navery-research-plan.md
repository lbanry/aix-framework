# The Navery UX Research Plan

## Objective

Determine whether customers understand, trust, and are willing to place a test order for a $59 personalized AI graduation poster, while measuring whether optional print and 3D keepsake upgrades deserve follow-up.

## Highest-Value Questions

1. Do buyers understand the offer before checkout?
2. Does $59 feel acceptable for a digital-first graduation poster with proofing and 2 revisions?
3. Which art style creates the most confidence and purchase intent?
4. Does "AI-generated" help, hurt, or need reframing as "personalized artwork with proofing"?
5. What information or reassurance is required before uploading photos?
6. Where does the intake form create friction or uncertainty?
7. Are optional physical or 3D keepsake upgrades interesting enough to quote manually?

## Phase 1: Five-Second Offer Clarity Test

Method: Show the first viewport for 5 seconds, then hide it.

Participants: 5-8 parents, grandparents, graduates, or gift buyers.

Ask:

- What is being sold?
- What do you think it costs?
- Is it digital, physical, or both?
- What would you click next?

Decision informed: Whether the hero and price block explain the offer clearly enough.

Pass signal: At least 6 of 8 participants can explain that this is a $59 custom digital graduation poster with optional physical upgrades.

## Phase 2: Style Confidence Test

Method: Show the four Celina Martin styles without long explanation.

Participants: 8-12 target buyers.

Ask:

- Which style would you choose as a gift?
- Which looks most premium?
- Which feels safest if this were for your graduate?
- Which would you avoid?
- What, if anything, makes the samples feel trustworthy or untrustworthy?

Decision informed: Default hero image, gallery ordering, style descriptions, and whether all four styles should remain in the MVP.

Pass signal: One or two styles produce clear preference patterns, and participants can choose without needing extra explanation.

## Phase 3: AI Trust And Expectation Interview

Method: Moderated 15-minute interview using the prototype and sample images.

Participants: 6-8 target buyers.

Ask:

- What does "AI-generated" make you expect?
- What worries you about using real photos?
- Would a digital proof and 2 revisions reduce concern?
- What wording would make this feel more trustworthy?
- What would make you ask for a refund or feel disappointed?

Decision informed: AI language, likeness disclaimers, revision policy, photo-permission copy, and FAQ priority.

Pass signal: Top trust objections are known and can be addressed with copy, proofing, or sample presentation.

## Phase 4: Intake Form Usability Test

Method: Ask users to complete a realistic test order using the static prototype.

Participants: 5-8 target buyers.

Observe:

- Where they pause.
- Whether they understand required vs. optional fields.
- Whether photo upload expectations are clear.
- Whether the permission checkbox creates concern.
- Whether upgrade checkboxes are understood as inquiry-based.

Decision informed: Form grouping, field labels, helper text, photo instructions, and upgrade wording.

Pass signal: Most users can complete the form without facilitator help and can explain what will happen after submission.

## Phase 5: Dummy Checkout Confidence Test

Method: Run users through intake and dummy checkout.

Participants: Same users as Phase 4 when possible.

Ask:

- Does the order summary match what you expected?
- Is "Place Test Order" clear without real payment?
- What would you need before paying for real?
- Would you expect a proof, a final file, or a physical product next?

Decision informed: Dummy checkout copy, order summary, final CTA, and readiness for real payment testing.

Pass signal: Users understand no real card is collected and can state the next step after the test order.

## Phase 6: Upgrade Interest Probe

Method: Ask upgrade questions after users understand the digital offer.

Participants: Same users as Phase 4 or post-submit survey respondents.

Ask:

- Would you want a physical 11x14 print?
- Would you want a magnet, keychain, coaster, or 3D keepsake?
- Which upgrade would you ask about first?
- Would upgrade interest depend on seeing the digital proof?

Decision informed: Whether upgrade inquiry should stay passive, move into follow-up email, or become a priced second-stage offer.

Pass signal: Upgrade interest is concentrated enough to justify quoting one or two upgrade types manually.

## MVP Metrics To Track

- Product page visits.
- Start order clicks.
- Style selections.
- Intake starts.
- Completed dummy checkouts.
- Most selected style.
- Upgrade interest rate.
- Digital-only acceptance rate.
- Questions or objections submitted in notes.
- Time from intake to first proof.
- Revision count per order.

## Recommended First Test

Run Phase 1 and Phase 2 first. They are fastest, require no new infrastructure, and directly inform whether the landing page and style gallery can support the MVP offer.

Then run Phase 4 and Phase 5 with the same participants to test whether interest survives the form and checkout path.
