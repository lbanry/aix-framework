# The Navery Current Research Summary

## Purpose

This summary gives The Navery a current external evidence base for deciding what to test first. It should guide hypotheses only. The Navery still needs first-party research with actual buyers before treating any finding as validated demand.

## Market And Gift Context

The personalized-gifts category is large and still growing. Research and Markets reports the global personalized gifts market growing from $30.79 billion in 2025 to $33.49 billion in 2026, with ecommerce, printing, engraving, and demand for unique memorable gifts listed as growth drivers.

Graduation gifting also appears to mix practical utility with sentimental keepsakes. Recent 2026 graduation gift coverage from The Guardian, Parents, Shutterfly, and specialty personalized-gift sellers repeatedly frames graduation as a milestone where buyers want encouragement, memory, and personal meaning, not only generic items.

Implication for The Navery: the market context supports testing a personalized graduation keepsake, but it does not prove that buyers will pay $59 for a digital-first AI poster. The MVP should test perceived value, clarity of deliverables, and confidence in the proofing process.

## AI Artwork Trust And Value Risk

External research on AI-generated art points to a material trust and valuation risk. Studies on AI art perception indicate that buyers may judge AI-created artwork differently than human-created work, especially when authorship, effort, likeness, or emotional value matter.

Personalization can improve perceived relevance and effort in AI-assisted art, but it should not be treated as a cure-all. Buyers may still need proofing, revision rights, examples, and clear limitations before they feel comfortable using real photos.

Implication for The Navery: "AI-generated" should be handled carefully. The strongest value frame is likely personalized keepsake artwork with a digital proof and two revisions, with AI described transparently but not allowed to create uncertainty about likeness guarantees.

## Ecommerce And Intake UX

Baymard checkout and form research is directly relevant because The Navery depends on a long intake form and a checkout-like commitment step. Their guidance emphasizes persistent labels, clear required and optional fields, natural typing flow, descriptive primary buttons, and avoiding unnecessary "Apply" actions in checkout flows.

Baymard also reports that many checkout experiences still perform poorly, and that checkout design can be a direct cause of abandonment. For The Navery, the risk is not only payment abandonment. It is also form abandonment caused by unclear photo requirements, upgrade wording, style selection, or uncertainty about what happens after submitting.

Implication for The Navery: the intake form should be tested as a conversion-critical flow. The order summary and dummy checkout must make it obvious that the test order is $59, digital-first, and does not collect real card details.

## Highest-Priority Research To Run

1. Offer clarity test: verify that buyers understand the product, price, digital-first delivery, proofing, revisions, and optional upgrades within seconds.
2. Style confidence test: identify which poster styles create the highest gift confidence and which create hesitation.
3. AI trust interview: learn whether "AI-generated" helps, hurts, or needs to be reframed.
4. Intake usability test: find friction in photo upload expectations, required fields, style choice, and permission language.
5. Dummy checkout confidence test: verify that the no-payment test order is understood and still feels credible.
6. Upgrade interest probe: measure whether print or 3D keepsake interest is strong enough to justify a manual quote workflow.
